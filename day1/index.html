🧠 Why did AlphaGo (2016) matter more than Deep Blue (1997) beating Kasparov at chess?
Deep Blue used brute-force computation + handcrafted rules — it was the Rule-Based era at scale. AlphaGo used deep reinforcement learning to develop intuition the programmers couldn't articulate. Go has more board positions than atoms in the universe — no rulebook could cover it. This proved deep learning could master domains where human-codified knowledge fails.
In-class game 2 of 3 Human Embedding Space
›
⏱ 10–12 minutes · teaches: embeddings, semantic similarity, vector space

Students physically become the vector space — proximity in the room = semantic similarity. No maths required.

  1. Clear a space. Trainer calls a word — students move to show similarity. People cluster near words they feel are related, move away from opposites.
  2. Round 1: King, Queen, Prince, Car, Bus, Dog, Cat, Apple, Orange, Samsung, Apple (the company) — watch "Apple" cluster differently depending on context
  3. Trainer calls "King minus Man plus Woman" — who do you stand near? (Answer: Queen)
  4. Round 2: Seoul, Tokyo, Samsung, Apple, Hunger, Tired, Happy, Fast, Slow
  5. Debrief: "This spatial relationship — similar meaning = nearby coordinates — is exactly what an embedding layer does inside every LLM. king − man + woman = queen is a real vector arithmetic result from Word2Vec."

Input Text
Your raw text prompt. "Translate this to Korean: Hello, how are you?" — everything you type becomes the model's context. The model processes the entire input simultaneously (unlike older RNNs that read one word at a time).
Example: "The best way to learn programming is to…"
Tokenizer
Text is split into tokens — subword units, not whole words. "unbelievable" → ["un", "believ", "able"]. GPT-4 has a vocabulary of ~100K tokens. You pay API cost per token. Context window limits are in tokens, not words (~750 words = ~1000 tokens).
Example: "Samsung Galaxy" → ["Sam", "sung", " Galaxy"] (3 tokens, not 2 words)
Embeddings + Positional Encoding
Each token is converted to a vector — a list of numbers (e.g. 768 numbers for BERT-base). Similar meanings → nearby vectors. "king" and "queen" have vectors much closer together than "king" and "refrigerator."

Positional encoding adds information about WHERE a token appears. Transformers process all tokens simultaneously, so without this they'd be order-blind — "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would look identical.
king − man + woman ≈ queen (real vector arithmetic from Word2Vec)
Multi-Head Self-Attention
Every token asks: "Which other tokens in this sequence matter most to me right now?" It computes a weighted sum over all other tokens — tokens that are contextually relevant get higher weights.

Example: In "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired" — self-attention figures out "it" refers to "animal", not "street", by assigning high attention weight from "it" to "animal".

Multi-head means this runs in parallel with different "perspectives" — one head may track grammar, another tracks entities, another tracks topic.
Query (Q): "what am I looking for?" · Key (K): "what do I contain?" · Value (V): "what do I give if attended to?"
Feed-Forward Layer
After attention, each token's representation passes through a fully-connected neural network independently. This is where most of the model's "knowledge" is stored — think of it as fact retrieval. The attention layer figures out context; the FFN layer retrieves relevant knowledge given that context.

GPT-4 has ~96 layers of attention + FFN stacked on top of each other. Each layer refines the representation further.
This stack is repeated N times (BERT-base: 12 layers, GPT-4: ~96 layers)
Output Token
The model outputs a probability score over its entire vocabulary (~100K tokens) for what the next token should be. It samples from the top candidates — controlled by temperature. Temperature = 0: always pick the highest probability (deterministic, boring). Temperature = 1: sample proportionally (creative, sometimes surprising).

Then it feeds that output back as new input and repeats — this is called autoregressive generation. That's why streaming text appears word by word.
"The capital of France is" → [Paris: 94%, Lyon: 2%, London: 1%, ...] → outputs "Paris"
In-class game 3 of 3 Human Autocomplete
›
⏱ 10–12 minutes · teaches: next-token prediction, temperature, context window

Students become the LLM — each person adds ONE word and passes it on. Physical demonstration of autoregressive generation.

  1. Trainer hands a student a post-it with a sentence starter: "The best way to learn machine learning is to…"
  2. That student says ONE word (next token). Pass to next student — they add one word. Continue around the room.
  3. Round 2: Trainer introduces "temperature" — tap a student's shoulder = they must give a random/unexpected word instead of the obvious one. Watch the sentence drift.
  4. Round 3: After 15 words, cover the post-it (simulate context window limit) — can students still continue coherently?
  5. Debrief: "Each word = one forward pass. Temperature = randomness knob. Forgetting the original sentence = context window. This is literally how GPT generates every response you've ever seen."

Diffusion Models — how AI generates images

Completely different from Transformers. They learn to reverse noise.

StepWhat happens
Forward (training)Take a real image → add tiny amounts of Gaussian noise iteratively → after ~1000 steps: pure static. The model learns what noise was added at each step.
Reverse (generation)Start with pure random noise → iteratively denoise, guided by a text prompt (via CLIP/T5 text encoder), until a coherent image emerges after ~20–50 steps.
Latent diffusionModern models (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 3) work in a compressed latent space — 64×64 instead of 512×512 pixels — then upscale at the end. This is why image generation takes seconds, not hours.

🧠 A Samsung engineer needs to build two features: (1) a chatbot that answers Galaxy Watch troubleshooting questions, (2) an AI that upscales SD photos to 4K on Samsung TVs. Which model architecture fits each feature — and why are they different?
(1) Troubleshooting chatbot → Transformer-based LLM (decoder-only, like GPT/Claude). Needs to understand natural language questions and generate text responses. (2) 4K upscaling → a form of diffusion/generative upscaling model, or a super-resolution CNN — it needs to generate realistic pixel content, not text. Samsung Vision AI uses exactly this approach on their Neo QLED TVs.

The Air Canada case (2024) — this is why oversight matters

Air Canada's AI chatbot told a customer he could buy a full-price bereavement ticket now and claim a retroactive discount later — a policy that didn't exist. Customer relied on it, paid full price, got denied the refund, sued. Court ruled: Air Canada is responsible for what its chatbot says.

Enterprise implication: When AI speaks on behalf of your company, your company owns the consequences — legally and reputationally. Every customer-facing AI deployment needs a human review gate for consequential outputs. This is the focus of Day 4's role-play exercise.
